Abstract

One of the standard velocity measurement methods used in the anemometer calibration process is the orifice method. It is used as a reference method for low velocity flows (below 1.5 m/s). Its application is based on theoretical relationships describing the use of flow restrictions in the measurement of mass flow rate and volume flow rate of liquids. The use of this method in the process of calibrating anemometers requires validation by referring the results to a higher order standard. This article presents the calibration process of a multi-hole orifice flow meter using a reference method in the form of an optical flow imaging technique that allows for the analysis of the flow velocity field. As a result of the work carried out, a series of measurement experiments were conducted using the spatial scanning method of the velocity profile with the PIV technique. Algorithms were implemented to determine the measurement uncertainty. Based on the results obtained, the flow coefficient of the orifice was determined along with the measurement uncertainty across the full range of flow velocities, enabling the validation of the orifice measurement method.

Knowledge Points

1. Motivation and Background for Low-Speed Wind Measurement

  • Low-speed measurement challenges (≤1.5 m/s)
  • Traditional pitot tubes struggle because dynamic pressure is extremely small at low velocities. Measurements become highly sensitive to slight temperature and air density changes, increasing uncertainty. Accurate low-speed reference velocities are essential for calibrating anemometers, especially hot-wire devices.
  • Orifice plate method as a solution
  • A multi-hole orifice plate narrows the flow path to create a measurable pressure difference even at low wind speeds. Enables calculation of reference flow velocity with stable pressure signals. Structural advantages: no moving parts, robust, minimal deterioration over time, making it attractive as a calibration standard.
  • Necessity of validation
  • The orifice method is non-standard (not defined in international standards), requiring validation. PIV is introduced to visualize the full flow field and provide high-fidelity velocity distributions for comparison. Purpose: determine the device-specific flow coefficient (FC) rigorously, enabling a reliable low-speed measurement standard.

2. Calibration of Hot-Wire Anemometers and Reference Standards

  • Hot-wire calibration context
  • Hot-wire anemometers require calibration against a correct reference wind speed to ensure coverage across ranges. Pitot-based calibration is viable only when dynamic pressure is sufficiently large; not suitable in ultra-low-speed regimes.
  • Constant-temperature anemometry and King’s Law
  • In constant-temperature systems, airflow cools the wire; circuitry supplies voltage to maintain constant wire temperature. The measured voltage correlates empirically with wind speed (King’s Law relationship). Calibration requires simultaneous measurement of reference wind speed and output voltage to establish the voltage–velocity function.
  • Use of dedicated calibrators
  • Small open-circuit wind tunnel devices (e.g., commercial probe calibrators like those offered by Canomax) serve as reference sources. Orifice plates within such calibrators generate pressure drops that can be empirically correlated with velocity, enabling reference wind speed generation for calibration.

3. Flow Coefficient (FC): Role, Determination, and Applicability

  • Reasons for determining FC
  • Corrects differences between theoretical models and reality (accounts for air viscosity, flow contraction due to narrowed paths). Essential variable for converting orifice-derived pressure differences to accurate flow velocity. In low-speed ranges, FC varies with velocity; determining FC across the entire range minimizes uncertainty and supports standardization to national-level reliability.
  • FC dependence and transferability
  • FC is a function of multiple physical parameters (e.g., opening area, diameters A0, At, nozzle/orifice geometry, wind tunnel characteristics). Correlation between pressure drop and velocity is manufacturer- and configuration-dependent; likely requires recalibration for different tunnels or plate shapes. Although functional forms (e.g., power-law-like) may be broadly consistent, the specific FC curve must be empirically established for each system.

Paper on a Low-Speed Calibrator for the Accurate Calibration of Sensors at Low Speeds Reporter: Kazuhi Sakai

Normally, when using relative measurement instruments such as hot-wire anemometers, a calibration process is required to establish the relationship between wind speed and output. This is done based on a reference wind speed obtained through absolute measurement. When the reference wind speed is sufficiently high, a Pitot tube is typically used to convert pressure measurements into dynamic pressure to determine the wind speed. Since this is an absolute measurement, it is frequently used as a reference to calibrate hot-wire anemometers. However, this paper focuses on a calibration wind tunnel designed to determine the correct reference wind speed for extremely low speeds—specifically, below 1.5 m/s.

The calibration wind tunnel itself is quite conventional, utilizing a nozzle-type expansion, a hood, and plates with multiple orifice-like holes to estimate wind speed from pressure differences. However, the study rigorously measures the achievable precision, using pressure sensors for pressure and PIV (Particle Image Velocimetry) for wind speed. The primary contribution of this paper lies in its quantitative evaluation of the resulting uncertainty.

Published in the journal Measurement, the scope of this paper differs significantly from those typically handled in our laboratory, as it reports on very meticulous estimations of measurement precision. The error estimation formulas—for example, Eq. (19)—are derived from the total derivatives of the evaluation variables; they lack scientific novelty and feel "textbook-like." Nevertheless, the paper is thorough in identifying sensitivities to influencing variables such as $p$, $T$, $T_d$, and $x_{CO2}$. While the content contributes to the high-precision enhancement of measuring instruments, there is little in the way of scientific novelty.

Mr. Sakai’s presentation was well-prepared, and I believe the content was explained carefully. Because the scope was slightly different from our usual topics, students asked several appropriate questions regarding the underlying assumptions and experimental conditions. It would have been better if the presenter, Mr. Sakai, had been able to provide more precise answers to those points.
