Paper Information

Paper Metadata

CFD simulations of wind flow and mean surface pressure for buildings with balconies: Comparison of RANS and LES

Authors: X. Zheng, H. Montazeri, B. Blocken Affiliation: Department of the Built Environment, Eindhoven University of Technology / Department of Civil Engineering, KU Leuven DOI: 10.1016/j.buildenv.2020.106747 Journal Name: Building and Environment Volume: 173 Pages: 106747 Year: 2020

Abstract

Façade geometrical details can substantially influence the near-façade airflow patterns and pressures. This is especially the case for building balconies as their presence can lead to multiple separation and recirculation areas near the façades and hence large changes in surface pressure distribution. Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has been widely used to investigate the impact of building balconies, mainly based on the steady Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) approach. The objective of the present study is to evaluate the performance of steady RANS and large-eddy simulations (LES) in predicting the near-façade airflow patterns and mean surface pressure coefficients (Cp) for a building with balconies for three wind directions θ = 0°, 90°, 180°.

Research Highlights

  • The study compares steady RANS and LES for near-façade airflow and mean surface pressure coefficients around a building with balconies under three wind directions.
  • Validation against wind-tunnel pressure data shows that both methods work reasonably well for the windward case, while LES is clearly more accurate for side and rear wind conditions.
  • RANS systematically underestimates pressure and wind speed inside balcony spaces, which can mislead ventilation and wind-environment design decisions.

Key Findings

  • At θ = 0°, both RANS and LES reproduce façade Cp reasonably well, with LES showing slightly lower average absolute deviation.
  • At θ = 90° and 180°, LES reproduces experimental Cp distributions much better, while RANS strongly underestimates the absolute pressure level.
  • RANS also predicts lower average and peak wind-speed ratios in the balcony zones, which could hide actual discomfort or safety risk.

Conclusion

LES shows consistently higher reliability than RANS for predicting façade pressure and near-façade airflow around balcony-equipped buildings. The gap is especially large for side and rear wind directions, where RANS systematically underestimates Cp and balcony wind speed. Relying only on RANS in design can therefore underestimate real ventilation and wind nuisance levels.

  • LES remained the most stable and accurate method across all three wind directions.
  • RANS showed the largest weakness for θ = 90° and 180°.
  • Using only RANS for balcony wind-environment design can create practical underestimation risk.

Comment

2026.04.10 Research on the computational accuracy of wall pressure around a building with balconies Presenter: Masuda Kyoshiro This paper discussed the reproducibility of wall-pressure coefficients around a high-rise building with balconies, comparing RANS and LES against wind-tunnel data. The central message was that LES reproduces pressure more accurately because it can better represent the fine balcony geometry. That emphasis did not feel especially essential, because it is already widely known that LES generally performs better than RANS in this type of calculation, a point also raised in the discussion. Still, since the paper was published in 2020, the amount of comparable data may have been smaller at that time. The presentation itself was well organized and easy to follow. The speaker seemed to understand the material well, and it worked effectively as a first meeting presentation for the newly joined students. Interesting / notable points - It is already a well-known fact that LES tends to outperform RANS in reproduction accuracy, so emphasizing only that point may not be the most essential contribution. - The underestimation of balcony interior wind speed by RANS is practically important, because it may lead to underestimation of safety issues in balcony wind-environment assessment.
