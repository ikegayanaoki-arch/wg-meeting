Deep Learning‐Based Urban Canopy Model for Complex Building Morphologies

Authors: Xinjie Meng, Yongwei Wang, Yuanjian Yang, Qian Huang, Jianping Guo, Yaning Lv, and Zekai Dai

Affiliation: Nanjing University of Information Science and Technology; Chinese Academy of Meteorological Sciences

DOI: 10.1029/2025MS005483
Journal: Journal of Advances in Modeling Earth Systems (JAMES), Volume 18, Pages e2025MS005483, 2026

Abstract: Deep learning offers new methodologies and tools for meteorological modeling, yet its application in urban canopy models remains nascent. This study develops an Urban Canopy Neural Network (UCNN), a deep learning‐based urban canopy model designed for complex building morphologies. When training the UCNN, the Town Energy Balance (TEB) model was used to simulate land‐atmosphere exchange fluxes of diverse building morphologies. We designed 5,000 unique combinations of six key building parameters in the TEB model. These simulations were driven by meteorological data from 10 urban flux tower sites across globally diverse regions, generating a total of 14,400,000 data samples. To evaluate generalization, the UCNN was tested against an independent set of observational flux data from 11 additional global sites. The UCNN demonstrated excellent generalization, achieving performance comparable to mainstream models while running approximately five times faster than the original TEB model.

Key Points

  • A new method was developed to build a deep learning‐based urban canopy model (UCNN) for complex building morphologies.
  • The model's generalization ability was validated using independent data from multiple diverse sites globally.
  • The deep learning‐based model achieves an accuracy level comparable to that of mainstream urban canopy models while improving computational speed by about five times.

Main Findings

  • Used the physical model (TEB) to generate 14.4 million data samples from 5,000 unique combinations of building parameters for training.
  • Demonstrated high accuracy comparable to traditional physical models in predicting radiation (shortwave/longwave) and heat fluxes (sensible/latent).
  • Proved that UCNN operates approximately five times faster than physical models, enabling significant reductions in computational costs.

Conclusion

This study developed and validated UCNN, a deep learning-based urban canopy model. By learning from TEB simulation results, UCNN can accurately and rapidly predict energy exchange between the surface and atmosphere under various building morphologies and climatic conditions. Validation against independent observational data showed that UCNN achieves accuracy comparable to major conventional physical models while being about five times faster. This demonstrates that deep learning can significantly contribute to the efficiency of meteorological simulations in complex urban environments.

Meeting Summary

In this meeting, a research presentation and Q&A session were held regarding the **Urban Canopy Neural Network (UCNN)**, a deep learning-based urban canopy model for complex building morphologies. ### 1. Research Background and Objectives Urban Canopy Models (UCMs) are essential for simulating energy exchange between the surface and the atmosphere. However, as cities become more complex, traditional physical models face high computational costs and difficulties in parameter settings. This study aims to build an efficient and highly versatile surrogate model (UCNN) by generating a dataset of diverse building morphologies using the physical model **TEB (Town Energy Balance)** and training a deep learning model on it. ### 2. Methodology and Dataset * **Data Generation:** 10 of 21 flux tower sites were used for training, and 11 sites were used for independent validation. Approximately 40.4 million training samples were generated from 5,000 random combinations of building morphologies. * **Model Architecture:** A structure combining **Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)** for spatial feature extraction and **Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU)** for time-series data processing is adopted. * **Input/Output:** Inputs include meteorological data and building parameters (height, albedo, etc.), and outputs are radiation and turbulence (Heat flux). ### 3. Key Q&A and Discussions * **Definition of Technical Terms:** The following terms were clarified during the meeting: * **Flux Tower:** Unlike regular weather stations, these facilities can simultaneously measure heat and momentum fluxes. * **Types of Radiation:** Difference between direct solar energy (shortwave) and energy emitted from surrounding objects (longwave). * **Types of Heat:** Distinction between "Sensible Heat," involving direct temperature changes, and "Latent Heat," used for phase changes like evaporation. * **Discussion on Accuracy Inversion:** A point of discussion was why UCNN, trained on data from a physical model (TEB), could be more accurate than the original TEB. * **Response:** It was explained that the loss function (MSE) might smooth out extreme values, eliminating noise and improving statistical accuracy. * **Concern:** However, it was pointed out that this smoothing property might make it **unsuitable for predicting extreme phenomena like typhoons**. * **Season and Location:** High radiation levels in winter data were confirmed to be due to the inclusion of sites in the **Southern Hemisphere**, such as Australia. ### 4. Conclusion and Future Plans This research demonstrated that deep learning models can function effectively as surrogates for physical models, achieving high computational efficiency (saving time and cost).
