Meeting Summary

On the assessment of pedestrian distress in urban winds

Created Date: April 15, 2026

Article Information

Title: On the assessment of pedestrian distress in urban winds

Authors: Giulio Vita, Zhenru Shu, Michael Jesson, Andrew Quinn, Hassan Hemida, Mark Sterling, Chris Baker

Affiliation: Department of Civil Engineering, University of Birmingham, Edgbaston, Birmingham, UK

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jweia.2020.104200

Journal Name: Journal of Wind Engineering & Industrial Aerodynamics

Volume: 203

Pages: 104200

Year: 2020

Abstract

Urban winds can cause a risk to pedestrian safety if not properly assessed. High-rise buildings produce a complex flow field at ground level, where regions of accelerated and recirculating flows are present. Gust wind speeds provide an indication of the maximal speed pedestrian might experience due to the unsteady flow. In this study, low- and high-fidelity numerical and experimental techniques to predict pedestrian level winds are tested on a realistic full-scale test route at the University of Birmingham campus during a storm event. Results show that it is beneficial to increase the complexity of simulations, as a direct correspondence exists between gust wind speed and the turbulent environment. While not much improvement is achieved by switching from Irwin probes to hot-wire anemometry, LES greatly outperforms RANS and challenges experimental simulations in terms of reliability. The validity of the peak factor is also questioned, and a general comment on the adequacy of each technique is discussed.

Research Highlights

  • Evaluates the risk posed to pedestrian safety by complex wind conditions around high-rise buildings in urban areas, including accelerated flows and recirculating flows.
  • Compares and validates low-fidelity and high-fidelity numerical simulations and experimental methods on an actual test route within the University of Birmingham campus under a full-scale storm event.
  • Clarifies the direct correlation between gust speed and the turbulent environment, and examines the validity of various methods for predicting pedestrian-level wind.

Main Findings

  • Large Eddy Simulation (LES) shows performance far superior to Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models and is comparable to experimental simulations in terms of reliability.
  • The improvement in accuracy obtained by switching from Irwin probes to hot-wire anemometry was limited, but LES is highly effective in capturing complex flow behavior.
  • Questions the validity of the conventional peak factor and suggests that the peak factor varies depending on the turbulent environment.

Conclusion

This study verified the effectiveness of various measurement and simulation methods for evaluating pedestrian wind safety environments in urban areas by comparing them with full-scale observational data. In the complex wind environment around high-rise buildings, it was shown that evaluating gusts, in addition to mean wind speed, is essential, and that the choice of method directly affects prediction accuracy. In particular, while the usefulness of LES as a high-accuracy numerical simulation was confirmed, it was concluded that caution is needed when using conventional simplified prediction methods and the peak factor.

  • For reliable evaluation of pedestrian wind environments, the adoption of high-fidelity numerical simulation methods such as LES is recommended over RANS.
  • Low-fidelity methods such as Irwin probes are suitable for predicting mean wind speed, but they have limitations in evaluating gusts in complex turbulent regions.
  • The peak factor used to evaluate pedestrian distress should be treated not as a constant value but as a dynamic variable that depends on the flow condition.

Meeting Highlights

04-14 Lecture: Risk-Based Pedestrian Wind Distress Assessment, Gust Metrics, and CFD/Wind Tunnel Validation

Presenter: Ali Farvardin

The lecture reviews risk-based design and assessment of pedestrian wind distress around buildings, motivated by the 2011 Bridgewater Place incident in Leeds that led to costly mitigation and stricter microclimate guidance in London and Leeds post-2019. Building on Baker (2015), it presents a loop-based methodology that quantifies pedestrian distress using effective wind speed, threshold exceedance probabilities, and safety criteria that vary by age, activity, wind typology, and gust duration. Distress thresholds and peak factors show substantial variability across literature, underscoring the need for standardized definitions of gust duration (1 s, 3 s, 10 s) and percentile levels (e.g., 99%) linked explicitly to time scales.

The session contrasts measurement and simulation techniques for mean and gust wind characterization. Low-fidelity tools like Irwin probes are practical for mean speed and longer-duration gusts but are limited for short, high-frequency fluctuations. High-fidelity methods—sonic anemometers, hot-wire anemometry, LDA, and PIV—capture turbulence and gusts better, though hot-wires (particularly single-wire) struggle with directionality in recirculation zones. The wind tunnel setup (open-circuit, 2 × 2 m cross-section, 14 m test length, up to 12 m/s) used Irwin probes (~50 Hz), hot-wires (~1000 Hz), and Cobra probes (250 Hz) on flat and topographic bases; height corrections between 1.2 m and 2 m were negligible. Field validation used Storm Ophelia (2017) data on the University of Birmingham campus: a 10 m-mast reference at a 32 m tower and eight ground-level sonic anemometers sampled at 10 Hz. Full-scale PSD followed a −5/3 inertial subrange and matched von Kármán spectra, supporting fidelity for CFD comparisons.

CFD studies evaluated domain strategies—truncated with roughness fetch, whole wind tunnel, and derived inlet—and turbulence models. RANS (k–ω SST primary; k–ε, k–ω, SA compared) generally predicted mean velocities reasonably but underpredicted TKE and ε, limiting gust representation. LES (WMLES on a medium grid for the truncated domain) better reproduced fluctuations, TKE, and dissipation profiles, aligning with high-fidelity measurements. Inflow generation and upstream development were shown to be critical; truncated fetches with surface roughness can grow turbulence from laminar starts, but sensitivity remains high, especially for RANS.

Comparisons along a pedestrian route revealed flow features: acceleration near corners (positions 1–2), followed by separated and recirculating regions (positions 3–6). LES matched mean and standard deviation trends best across all eight points, capturing rich recirculation-driven gust structure. RANS performed acceptably near acceleration zones but underpredicted mean and fluctuations downstream, missing recirculation and vortical “break-in” features. Irwin probes tracked overall mean trends and longer-duration gusts but deviated at higher frequencies; hot-wires captured high-frequency content better yet were unreliable in strongly directional recirculation due to sensor orientation limits. PSD analyses clarified instrument bandwidth suitability, with markers linking 3 s and 10 s gust frequencies.

Gust estimation was addressed both directly (from high-frequency measurements and LES) and indirectly via peak factor relations UG = U + G × σU. Full-scale and experimental results showed that G varies spatially and with local flow conditions, tending to be lower in accelerated high-mean-speed areas and higher where mean speeds are lower but turbulence is strong. A single universal peak factor is inappropriate; G should depend on local σu, TKE, and mean wind speed. Spatial analyses indicated that gust risk is not confined to high-mean-speed zones—recirculation regions can exhibit high gustiness despite lower mean flow. Flat vs. topographic wind tunnel bases produced modest differences (typically ~3–15%) in mean and gust metrics, allowing subsequent analyses to rely on flat-base data for practicality.

Practically, high-fidelity measurements and LES provide the most reliable basis for gust-related pedestrian distress assessments but are resource-intensive. RANS and Irwin probes remain prevalent in routine regulatory studies due to cost and the need to assess multiple wind directions with occurrence weighting, despite known underprediction of fluctuations in complex flows. The lecture notes evolving regulatory contexts (including references to AIJ-related standards and widespread RANS/wind-tunnel use) and emphasizes careful post-processing, rigorous validation against full-scale events, and context-specific threshold selection to manage uncertainty.

Comments

Paper on evaluation methods for wind speed at pedestrian height, gust evaluation methods, and prediction accuracy of RANS vs. LES.

Reporter: Ali Farvardin

This paper compares methods for evaluating strong winds (gusts) in wind environment assessment using outdoor observations, LES, and RANS. The study evaluates the 99th percentile wind speed for time-series data with 1-second, 3-second, and 10-second moving averages. It compares RANS estimates (with a constant peak factor), direct LES estimates, direct estimates from outdoor observation data, and wind tunnel experiment results using Irwin sensors and hot-wire anemometers. The results demonstrated that hot-wire anemometry and LES are superior to RANS in representing the peak factor observed in outdoor measurements.

Comparing against actual outdoor measurement data is particularly valuable. However, the domain settings for RANS and the inflow settings for LES were somewhat unclear. In particular, the inflow wind settings seemed quite different from the experimental conditions, making it almost expected that the outdoor observation results could not be perfectly reproduced.

Mr. Ali's presentation was excellent, and the paper contained summaries of many studies, which I believe he reviewed very thoroughly. There were many questions from students, which was great. Students who did not ask a question this time should definitely prepare one for the next session.

Interesting Points

  • Using the 99th percentile wind speed as the evaluation metric.
  • Using moving averages for gust evaluation. For comparison with outdoor observations, time-scale conversion is essential, so it is necessary to convert LES and wind tunnel data using similar methods.
  • While it may seem self-evident that the peak factor varies at acceleration points, this point was accurately demonstrated through a very simple and clear analysis.
