Abstract

Using field data from a coastal site, we explored the turbulence spectra of boundary layer flows over land and sea, focusing on the occurrence and characteristics of the -1 spectral scaling in the energy production region. Results reveal that the interaction of the flow with surface roughness is a critical factor influencing the extent of the -1 scaling. Flows over the sea under generally calm conditions consistently exhibited a robust -1 spectral scaling across times of day and seasons. In contrast, flows over land, encountering higher surface roughness and heterogeneity, display very limited -1 scaling. Inspection of turbulence intermittency showed sea winds with slightly higher intermittency, though both remain lower. Also, spectral normalization using the integral wave number provides a general trend in the spectra well across heights and flow conditions onto a general trend, highlighting the potential role of the integral scale in spectral behavior. These insights maybe instrumental for atmospheric modeling, wind energy systems, and pollutant dispersion.

Research Points

  • Investigated the impact of surface roughness in coastal regions on the turbulent wind spectrum, particularly the "-1 power law" scaling, by comparing conditions over land and sea.
  • Observed winds over different surfaces (sea and land) using data from a meteorological observation tower installed on the Yucatán Peninsula in Mexico.
  • Analyzed the turbulent characteristics of sea and land winds under calm meteorological conditions, focusing on the occurrence and features of the "-1 power law" scaling in the energy spectrum.

Main Findings

  • Over the sea, due to the influence of the smooth sea surface, the "-1 power law" was consistently observed regardless of time of day or season. This is because large-scale vortex structures are easily maintained.
  • Over land, due to higher surface roughness and heterogeneity, the appearance of the "-1 power law" was limited, suggesting that the coherence of turbulent structures was disrupted.
  • Sea winds showed a slightly higher intermittency (larger fluctuations) compared to land winds, but overall fluctuations remained low for both. Furthermore, spectral normalization using the integral wavenumber revealed a universal trend across different heights and flow conditions.

Conclusion

(Summary)
It was shown that surface roughness is a critical factor determining the presence or absence of the "-1 power law" in the turbulent spectra of the atmospheric boundary layer. Winds over smooth seas consistently exhibit the "-1 power law", while those over rough land show limited occurrence. This difference contributes to improving the accuracy of wind energy and atmospheric pollutant dispersion predictions.

(Bullet points)

  • The interaction with the surface is a crucial factor determining the appearance and extent of the "-1 power law" in the turbulent energy spectrum.
  • Over smooth and homogeneous seas, large-scale coherent vortex structures are dominant, and a robust "-1 power law" is consistently observed.
  • Over rough and heterogeneous land, the "-1 power law" is rarely observed, indicating that surface complexity disrupts the coherence of large-scale turbulent structures.

2. Details of the Paper Presentation: Surface Roughness and Wind Spectral Characteristics

This study focuses on the "-1 power law" scaling in the energy-containing range. While the Kolmogorov "-5/3 power law" is well-known for atmospheric boundary layer spectra, this research investigates the characteristics of the preceding range.

* Observation Conditions and Data Selection:
* Approximately 30 samples were selected from a meteorological observation tower.
* The analysis focused exclusively on neutral stability conditions, which refer to a state where the heat exchange (heat flux) between the ground and the atmosphere is nearly zero.
* Significant Differences between Sea Wind and Land Wind:
* Under sea wind conditions, the region exhibiting the "-1 power law" was significantly broader and more clearly defined compared to land wind.
* This is attributed to the sea surface being much smoother than land (e.g., vegetation).
* Robustness of Characteristics:
* Time of Day: The -1 region was consistently observed throughout the day, regardless of morning, afternoon, evening, or night.
* Season: Similar scaling characteristics were maintained throughout the year, including data from February, April, October, and December.
* Transition Indicator: The characteristic frequency ($f_u$), which is the reciprocal of the integral time scale, consistently lies within this -1 region, indicating the onset of a transition from large-scale to small-scale structures.

3. Discussion on Analysis Methods and Physical Interpretation

The meeting included a deep discussion on why this scaling develops in smooth sea winds and how to eliminate the influence of altitude.

* Influence of Altitude and Normalization:
* As measurement altitude increases, the -1 region tends to expand towards lower frequencies.
* However, by converting frequencies to wavelengths and normalizing them using the mean wind speed ($U$) and integral length scale ($K_I$) at each altitude, it was confirmed that the peak positions align, and a consistent trend is obtained even under different altitudes and wind conditions (sea/land).
* Interpretation based on the "Attached Eddy Hypothesis":
* "Attached eddies" constrained by the wall (ground surface) follow the energy cascade ($-5/3$ power law), but in layers where "detached eddies", which are unaffected by the ground, are dominant, energy is uniformly distributed across each frequency, and the spectrum becomes flat ($-1$ region).
* It is thought that sea winds, due to their lower roughness, exhibit the characteristics of these detached eddies more strongly, resulting in a wider -1 region.
* System Complexity and Intermittency:
* Analysis using structure functions and fractal dimensions suggested that sea winds exhibit higher intermittency and are more complex and difficult to predict as a system than land winds.

4. Observation Environment and Technical Details

Presenter: Yin Sankag
  • This paper analyzed a characteristic of spectra observed in coastal outdoor observation data, where the energy intensity attenuates with the inverse of frequency (a -1 power law). This means that for each frequency, energy is uniformly distributed, creating a plateau region where no energy cascade occurs.
  • Observations were conducted in a coastal region of Mexico, combining 3D and 2D ultrasonic anemometers on a tower at heights of 4m, 12m, 20m, and 50m, measuring wind speed on a daily basis. A total of 20 datasets were analyzed.
  • In boundary layer flows, eddies influenced by the surface, known as attached eddies, undergo energy scaling due to the field of view, where energy is exchanged from large eddies to small eddies. In this case, the spectrum exhibits a -5/3 power law decay. On the other hand, in cases like flows over the sea, where the surface roughness is sufficiently small and the boundary number is large, detached eddies, which are not influenced by the surface, allow energy to be uniformly distributed across various frequencies. As a result, when viewed in spectral space, energy attenuates with the inverse of frequency (a -1 power law), meaning a region with uniform energy intensity (F-type energy intensity) becomes active. This region corresponds to a transition region where the energy cascade is about to occur.
