History of the peak three-second gust

Authors: Franklin T. Lombardo

Affiliation: Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, United States

DOI: 10.1016/j.jweia.2020.104447
Journal: Journal of Wind Engineering & Industrial Aerodynamics, Volume, 208, Pages 104447, 2021

Abstract: This paper reviews the historical background of the term "three-second gust" commonly used in the field of wind engineering. It aims to clarify misunderstandings about the origin of this term, outlining its evolution, the rationale behind its selection, and considerations for its future use.

Key Points of the Research

  • The origin of the "three-second gust" is primarily based on perceptions of anemometer response characteristics.
  • Three main fields—aviation, meteorology, and wind engineering—have contributed to the history and current usage of the term "three-second gust."
  • The process by which the WMO (World Meteorological Organization) eventually came to recommend a 3-s average for the peak gust standard.

Main Findings

  • The concept of a "three-second gust" is largely a non-technical construct, born more from past engineers' assumptions about the characteristics of observing instruments (especially the Dines anemometer) than from rigorous physical principles.
  • Aerodynamic characteristics of buildings were also considered to justify this concept retroactively, but they were not the original primary reason.
  • Modern observation systems can capture gusts of shorter time scales, so the "three-second" standard may underestimate the impact of gusts, especially on smaller structures.

Conclusion

This study reveals that the term "three-second gust" is largely a non-technical construct, deriving significantly from historical perceptions of anemometer system response characteristics. Its adoption was driven by the cooperation of multiple scientific and technical communities and standardization by the WMO. However, while the concept was partially justified retroactively by the aerodynamic characteristics of buildings, its origins are not based on strict physical laws. With the advancements in modern measurement technology allowing for the accurate recording of shorter-duration gusts, the fixed three-second standard may underestimate the maximum wind loads on smaller structures. Therefore, a re-evaluation of gust definition and measurement methods is warranted in future research and practice.

1. Definition and Background of 3-Second Average Wind Speed

  • Peak gust ($G$) is generally defined as the "maximum value of the 3-second average wind speed" and is calculated using the gust factor ($G_u$) and the 1-hour or 10-minute average wind speed.
  • The main reason for choosing the "3-second" duration is based on the response performance of anemometers and recording systems of the time (physical limitations of analog recorders) and the aerodynamic characteristics of obstacles such as buildings.
  • There is a background that past measurement systems could not record high-frequency turbulence, and it was physically possible to capture only fluctuations of about 2 to 3 seconds.

2. Technical Aspects of Filtering and Moving Average

  • Performing a moving average is an operation in the time domain, but in the frequency domain, it is synonymous with filtering that cuts high-frequency components.
  • Mathematically, it corresponds to filtering using the "sinc function", and the longer the averaging time ($T$), the more fine fluctuations are truncated.
  • Although the current observation system in Japan generally samples at 0.25-second intervals and outputs a 3-second moving average, it has been pointed out that historically it was merely treating the maximum output of the instrument as the "3-second value".

3. Engineering Validity and Issues

  • Practical Convention: The 3-second standard was adapted from knowledge in the aviation field, and it is widely accepted as a practical convention rather than having a strong technical basis.
  • Effect of Structure Size: While a 3-second duration may be sufficient for small-scale buildings, it may be too long for smaller structures and cladding, and a re-evaluation of the definition is necessary.
  • Large-Scale Structures: Conversely, for large-scale structures such as huge towers, it may be necessary to consider wind speeds of longer duration.
  • Future Proposals: For research purposes, it is recommended to archive high-frequency data (time-series data) and use a "gust duration" appropriate for the object, rather than relying on a fixed 3-second average.
